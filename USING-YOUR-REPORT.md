# Using Your Report

Your report is a structured competitive intelligence brief built from what a company has actually said on YouTube over the past two and a half years. This guide explains what each section tells you, what to look for, and how to use the report before a meeting.

---

## How the report is structured

Every report opens with an **executive summary** — four sentences that give you the strategic picture before you read anything else. Then ten sections follow, each answering a specific question about the company.

The report is designed to be read top-to-bottom before a meeting, or section-by-section when you're preparing a specific argument or training session.

---

## Reading each section

### Executive summary

The executive summary is the one thing you'd tell a colleague if they asked "what's the deal with this company?" in a hallway. It leads with what the company is trying to do that they haven't fully said out loud — the strategic intent behind the content, not the content itself.

**What to look for:**
- A clear directional signal (are they expanding, consolidating, pivoting?)
- Something that surprised you — the executive summary is designed to surface the non-obvious

### Product line

An inventory of every product or product family mentioned across the channel, ranked by how many videos reference it. Signal strength (the number in parentheses) tells you which products are getting the most airtime — not just which ones exist.

**What to look for:**
- Products with high signal that you didn't expect
- Products that are conspicuously absent from the channel (they may be de-emphasized or in trouble)
- New entries in recent videos vs. products that disappeared

### The problem they're obsessed with

The customer pain driving what they build — stated as outcomes, not features. High signal strength here (10+ videos) means this isn't marketing copy; it's a deeply held belief about what the market needs.

**What to look for:**
- Whether the problem they claim to solve matches what their customers actually say in the same report (see: Who they're building for)
- Whether the problem framing has shifted over time (see: What's shifted)

### Who they're building for

The customer archetype and job-to-be-done — derived from customer voices and problems across the channel, not from the company's stated target market. This is who they're *actually* building for, which is sometimes different from who they say they are.

**What to look for:**
- The "not building for" statement — a deliberate exclusion is a strategic positioning choice
- Whether the archetype is specific (a signal of product clarity) or vague (a signal of trying to please everyone)

### How they decide what to build

The revealed principle behind their product decisions — not what they say their process is, but what the pattern of what they've built and chosen not to build actually shows.

**What to look for:**
- An explicit "we won't do X" — this is a constraint worth understanding; is it a strength or a blind spot?
- Whether their stated principle matches the product line (does what they ship match what they claim to believe?)

> *[Dean — your take on product philosophy as competitive signal. What does it mean when a company's revealed decisions don't match their stated philosophy?]*

### Where they're placing bets

What they're investing in, stated as a belief: "they believe X will be true, so they're building Y." The underlying assumption matters as much as the bet itself — if the assumption turns out to be wrong, the bet fails.

**What to look for:**
- Bets that rely on an assumption you believe is wrong — that's a competitive opening
- Bets that validate something you're also doing — that's market confirmation
- Bets that are missing from what you'd expect given their problem obsession

### How they frame the category

Are they creating a new category, or reframing an existing one to escape comparison? Category framing is one of the most strategic choices a company makes — it determines who they're implicitly competing with and what they don't have to answer for.

**What to look for:**
- Whether the framing is genuinely new or just new language for an old thing
- What their framing *protects them from having to say* — that absence is often as telling as the framing itself

### Momentum and tone

An assessment of where the company's energy is right now — on offense or defense, confident or hedging, accelerating or consolidating. This isn't about what they're saying; it's about *how* they're saying it.

**What to look for:**
- "On defense" signals: justification language, parity claims, overemphasized differentiation
- "On offense" signals: invitation language, new customer archetypes, ecosystem expansion
- Hedging language around a specific product or bet is often a signal that something isn't working

### What's shifted

The temporal section — what changed between the earlier videos in the window and the more recent ones. Messaging shifts are often more telling than the current messaging, because they show direction and momentum.

**What to look for:**
- Something that disappeared entirely — that's usually a strategic retreat
- Something that became prominent recently — that's usually a signal of a new direction or a new concern
- Whether the shift is accelerating or gradual

### What they're not saying

The most analytically demanding section — and the highest-signal one. This is absence reasoning: what would a company in this category normally say that this company hasn't said? What does that silence reveal?

**What to look for:**
- A gap that looks like a vulnerability (they're not talking about X because X is a weakness)
- A gap that looks like a deliberate positioning choice (they're not talking about X because they don't want to invite that comparison)
- A gap that looks like an announcement they haven't made yet (they're circling something without naming it)

## Before a meeting: a quick checklist

- [ ] Read the executive summary — what's the one thing you want to verify or challenge in the meeting?
- [ ] Check the product line — are there products you didn't know about?
- [ ] Read "What they're not saying" — what question could you ask that they'd find uncomfortable to answer?
- [ ] Check "Momentum and tone" — are they on offense or defense right now? That changes how you position yourself.
- [ ] Check "What's shifted" — what changed recently? Fresh context matters more than old framing.

---

## A note on evidence

Every claim in the report is backed by a verbatim quote from a specific video, with the video title and date. If a claim surprises you, you can trace it back to the source.

The signal strength number (e.g., *signal: 12 videos*) tells you how many distinct videos support that claim. A claim with signal:12 is a pattern. A claim with signal:1 is a data point — useful, but treat it as such.

"What they're not saying" is the exception: those claims are analytical inference about absence, not quotes. They're clearly labeled and are meant to prompt questions, not assert facts.
